Follow the Money

With the coming 2016 election, it is important for us to be aware of what could be driving our future president's decisions. One major motivator in politics is money. Thus, as the average citizen, it is important for us know who or what is supporting your politician financially.

Since the turn of the 20th century, two landmark events have forcefully changed the United States's campaign financing landscape. The 2000 Presidential Election showed that it was possible to raise large amounts of funds without the help of the Federal Election Commission (FEC). Also, the Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission ruling saw the rise of Super PACS. These two events have completely flipped where politicians raise money.

We will look at the impact of these two events by following the money.

Campaign Finance 101

The modern presidential political campaign is almost impossible to personally finance. To put things into perspective, the 2008 cycle saw presidential candidates spending $2.4 billion in total. So, politicians look to a variety of sources for additional financial support:

  1. The National Party Committee the candidate is affiliated with (e.g. The Democratic or Republican National Committees)
  2. Political Action Committees (e.g Citizens United), organizations that campaign for or against candidates, ballot initiatives, or other types of legislation.
  3. Individuals (e.g. you)
  4. The Federal Election Committee (FEC), if the candidate stays under specific spending limits

To avoid corruption of our officials, special limits on contributions are put in place to ensure that a single person or entity does not contribute a majority of a candidate's funds.

2000 Presidential Election

Prior to 2000, presidential candidates relied heavily on public funding. The FEC matched a candidate's funds if they stuck to certain spending limits. Candidates begrudgingly accepted these limits, seeing the FEC's matching as a good deal.

In the 2000 election, GOP frontrunners Steve Forbes and George W. Bush both refused the matching funds so that they could blow past the spending limits in order to gain an advantage over the other. The 2000 cycle saw Bush raising over $70 million, a number previously thought impossible. The bar was raised, and the continued arms race ushered in a new era of campaign finance.

The close election exposed a loophole where party committees could contribute unlimited funds to candidates. The 2002 Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act fixed that and all was well with the world.

Citizens United v. Federal Election Committee

With the party committee loophole closed and the fight for campaign funds continuing to escalate, candidates looked to PACs to augment their warchests. In the 2008 election, candidates used another loophole, using independent expenditure PACs to bypass contribution limits and quickly raise campaign funds.

In 2010, the Supreme Court supported this practice by ruling that the prohibition of all independent expenditures violated the First Amendment. As long as PACs do not directly contribute to or coordinate with a candidate, they are able to receive and spend unlimited amounts of money.

In essence, the ruling ensured that wealthy individuals, corporations, and other organizations can sidestep donation limits by creating and contributing to PACs. Since the ruling, PAC spending has skyrocketed.

How does this affect you?

Presidential candidates have to raise more to keep from being left behind, but must do so in the same amount time. While online donating has made it easier than ever for candidates to reach the masses, it is more efficient to land the proverbial big fish. To overcome contribution limits, candidates are looking increasingly to PACs.

While the number of contributions <$500 has increased significantly, these contributions are making up less and less of the overall fundraising landscape.

On the other hand, contributions over $2600 (over the individual contribution limit) has increased from 37% to 50% of all contributions.
