Following the Money by Alan Si, Doug Kang

With the coming 2016 election, it is important for us to be aware of what could be driving our future president's decisions. One major motivator in politics is money. To avoid corruption of our officials, special limits on contributions exist to ensure that a single person or entity does not contribute a majority of a candidate's funds.

Total Contributions to Presidential Elections

Since the turn of the 20th century, two landmark events have forcefully changed the United States's campaign financing landscape. The 2000 Presidential Election showed that it was possible to raise large amounts of funds without the help of the Federal Election Commission (FEC). Additionally, the Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission ruling saw the increased use of Political Action Committees (PACs) for unlimited fundraising and spending.

These two events were the catalyst for a dangerous new trend in campaign fundraising. The wealthy are using PACs to bypass contribution limits and unduly influence our candidates

We will look at the impact of these two events by following the money.

Campaign Finance 101

The modern presidential political campaign is almost impossible to personally finance. So, politicians look to a variety of sources for additional financial support:

  1. The National Party Committee the candidate is affiliated with (e.g. The Democratic or Republican National Committees)
  2. Political Action Committees (e.g Citizens United), organizations that campaign for or against candidates, ballot initiatives, or other types of legislation.
  3. Individuals (e.g. you)
  4. The Federal Election Committee (FEC), if the candidate stays under specific spending limits

2000 Presidential Election

Prior to 2000, presidential candidates relied heavily on public funding. The FEC matched a candidate's funds if they stuck to certain spending limits. Candidates begrudgingly accepted these limits, seeing the FEC's matching as a good deal.

However, in the 2000 election, GOP frontrunners Steve Forbes and George W. Bush refused the matching funds so that they could ignore spending limits and gain an advantage over the other. The 2000 cycle saw Bush raising over $70 million, a number previously thought impossible. The bar was raised, and the arms race ushered in a new era of campaign finance.

Citizens United v. Federal Election Committee

With candidates having to raise more money to keep from being left behind, candidates looked to PACs to augment their warchests. In the 2008 election, candidates used independent expenditure PACs to bypass contribution limits and quickly raise campaign funds.

In 2010, the Supreme Court supported this practice by ruling that the prohibition of all independent expenditures violated the First Amendment. As long as PACs do not directly contribute to or coordinate with a candidate, they are able to receive and spend unlimited amounts of money.

In essence, the ruling ensured that wealthy individuals, corporations, and other organizations can sidestep donation limits by creating and contributing to PACs.

How does this affect you?

While online donating has made it easier than ever for candidates to reach the masses, it is more efficient to land the proverbial big fish. To bypass contribution limits, wealthy donors and candidates are looking increasingly to PACs.

While the number of contributions less than $1000 has increased significantly, these contributions are making up less and less of the overall fundraising landscape.

Contributions under $1000 have dropped dramatically since 1992, from contributing 57% of all campaign donations to 27% in just 10 years.
